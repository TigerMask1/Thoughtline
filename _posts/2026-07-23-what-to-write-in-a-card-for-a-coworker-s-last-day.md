Staring at a blank farewell card while it circulates through the office—or pops up as a link in your inbox—is surprisingly high-stress. You want to avoid sounding like a robotic automated email, but you also might not know the person well enough to write a long personal note.

To strike the right balance, stick to a simple three-part formula: acknowledge their contribution, mention a specific project or positive quality, and wish them well in their next step. If appropriate, add a way to stay in touch.

Here are specific message templates tailored to different workplace relationships, so you can sign the card with confidence and move on with your workday.

## For a Close Work Friend
When you have shared hundreds of lunches, coffee runs, and stressful deadlines, keep the tone warm and personal. Mention a shared memory or an inside joke from a real project.

* "Work is going to be about 80% less fun without our morning coffee debriefs. Thank you for keeping me sane during the website migration. Let's get dinner next week!"
* "I'm so happy for your new role, but selfishly terrified to survive quarterly reporting without you. Don't be a stranger—text me as soon as you settle in."
* "You've been the best desk neighbor and an even better friend. Congratulations on taking this huge next step!"

## For a Manager or Mentor
When writing to a supervisor, focus on gratitude for their leadership, specific guidance, or professional development support. Acknowledging their impact helps maintain a strong [professional network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Professional_network) for future references.

* "Thank you for all your support over the past two years. Your feedback during the Q3 client review made a huge difference in my career growth. Wishing you all the best in your next chapter!"
* "I really appreciate how much time you took to help me navigate my role here. Your leadership set a great example for the whole department. Good luck with the new position!"
* "It has been a genuine privilege working on your team. Thank you for advocating for us and always encouraging our ideas."

## For Someone You Didn't Work With Closely
If you only interacted with the departing employee occasionally in cross-functional meetings or passed them in the breakroom, keep your message brief, polite, and positive.

* "It was great working alongside you on the planning committee last fall. Best of luck in your next role!"
* "Thanks for always being so helpful whenever I reached out with tech questions. Wishing you all the best moving forward."
* "Congratulations on the new opportunity! It was a pleasure having you on the team."

## For Remote or Hybrid Teammates
When your relationship exists mostly through video calls and messaging apps like [Slack](https://slack.com), acknowledge the digital collaboration style you shared.

* "I will miss seeing your pet pop up on video calls and your quick problem-solving in chat! Thanks for being such a reliable remote partner on the redesign."
* "Working across three time zones was always easier with you on the team. Best of luck at your new gig!"
* "Even though we only met in person twice, you were a huge part of my daily work. Let's definitely stay connected on [LinkedIn](https://www.linkedin.com)!"

## Practical Rules for Signing Group Cards
To make sure the card signing process goes smoothly for everyone involved, follow three basic etiquette rules:

* **Watch your physical footprint:** If 15 to 20 people need to sign a paper greeting card, keep your message to 2 or 3 sentences. Taking up half a page leaves late signers with no room.
* **Pass it along quickly:** Don't let a physical card folder sit on your desk for hours. Sign it within 15 minutes and hand it to the next person so the organizer isn't scrambling at 4:30 PM on the person's final day.
* **Keep it positive:** Even if someone is leaving under tense circumstances or after a tough project, a farewell card is not the venue for passive-aggressive remarks or jokes about company management.
