Starting pottery is exciting, but it quickly becomes an expensive hobby. If your coworker has recently signed up for wheel-throwing or hand-building classes at a local community studio, they’ve likely realized two things: it’s incredibly fun, and it requires a surprising amount of gear. 

When buying a gift for a beginner potter, avoid purchasing raw clay or glazes. Studios usually have strict rules about what materials can go into their kilns to prevent accidental meltdowns. Instead, focus on tools that upgrade their daily studio experience. Here are five practical, highly-rated gifts under $30 that any new potter will actually use.

## Flexible Polymer Ribs
Most beginners start with a cheap, generic wood or metal scraper (called a rib) that comes in a standard starter pack, often made by legacy brands like [Kemper Tools](https://kempertools.com/). While these basic tools work, they lack the flexibility needed for fine shaping.

A polymer rib from a brand like [Mudtools](https://mudtools.com/) is a massive upgrade. These ribs are made of flexible, medical-grade materials that help smooth clay on the wheel or shape hand-built coils. 

* **What to buy:** The Mudtools Red (very soft) or Yellow (soft) polymer rib in "Shape 1" or "Shape 0".
* **Why it's great:** It conforms to the curve of the hand and the clay, making it much easier to pull smooth walls without gouging the piece.
* **Cost:** Around $8 to $10 each. You can easily buy a set of two different flexibilities for under $20.

## A Split-Leg Canvas Apron
Throwing clay on a spinning wheel is a messy business. Standard kitchen aprons ride up or don't cover the knees when sitting down, leaving the inner thighs and pants covered in wet clay splatter.

A split-leg apron (sometimes called a French-style or potter's apron) is designed specifically for sitting at the wheel. It has a split down the middle that straps around each leg individually, keeping pants completely clean while allowing full range of motion.

* **What to buy:** Look for a heavy-duty canvas split apron with adjustable straps and deep pockets.
* **Why they'll love it:** It saves them from driving home from the studio in damp, clay-stained clothes.
* **Cost:** Highly-rated canvas options on major online retail sites generally cost between $22 and $28.

## Heavy-Duty Hand Care Kit
Clay is essentially microscopic rock dust, and it acts as a natural desiccant. Working with wet clay for hours strips the skin of its natural oils, leaving potters with dry, cracked, and chalky hands. Normal scented lotions simply won't cut it.

* **What to buy:** A tin of O'Keeffe's Working Hands cream or a classic jar of lanolin-based salve like Bag Balm.
* **Bonus addition:** Pair the hand cream with a 10-pack of compressed cellulose sponges. Potters use sponges constantly to clean their wheel, wipe down bats, and absorb excess water from the inside of tall vessels.
* **Cost:** $15 to $18 for both the cream and a pack of sponges.

## A Waterproof Pottery Logbook
In pottery, keeping track of your process is vital. A beginner needs to note down which clay body they used, how much a piece weighed before throwing, what glaze combination they applied, and how it looked after the final firing. Because clay studios are wet, dusty environments, normal paper notebooks quickly warp and disintegrate.

* **What to buy:** A pocket-sized waterproof notebook, such as a "Rite in the Rain" notebook, paired with a fine-tip permanent marker.
* **Why they'll love it:** It allows them to sketch designs and record glaze recipes right at the damp studio table without worrying about wet hands ruining their notes.
* **Cost:** $10 to $15.

## A Wiggle Wire Cutter
To get a finished pot off the wooden or plastic wheel head (called a bat), potters slide a wire cutter underneath the base. A standard straight wire cutter does the job, but a "wiggle wire" (a wire cutter with a wavy, crimped pattern) leaves an elegant, wavy texture on the bottom of the pot.

* **What to buy:** A Mudtools "Wiggle Wire" or similar wavy clay cutter.
* **Why it's great:** It allows the user to create beautiful, professional-looking textured bottoms on their mugs and bowls without needing advanced carving skills. It is incredibly satisfying to use.
* **Cost:** Around $10 to $14.
