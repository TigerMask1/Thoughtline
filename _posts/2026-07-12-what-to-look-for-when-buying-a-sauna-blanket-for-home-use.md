If you want the sweat and relaxation of a traditional sauna but don't have the space or budget for a wooden cabin, an infrared sauna blanket is the most practical alternative. These sleeping-bag-like devices use [infrared radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared) to heat your body directly rather than heating the air around you. However, because you will be lying directly inside this heated envelope for 30 to 60 minutes at a time, you need to be highly selective about what you buy.

## Low EMF (Electromagnetic Field) Ratings

Every electronic device emits some level of electromagnetic fields (EMF), but because a sauna blanket wraps completely around your body, minimizing this exposure is critical.

Look for manufacturers that explicitly advertise "low EMF" or "ultra-low EMF" and provide specific testing numbers. Ideally, the blanket should emit less than 3 milligauss (mG) of EMF when measured directly on the surface. High-quality brands like [HigherDOSE](https://higherdose.com) use shielded heating coils to keep these levels near zero. If a brand does not mention EMF levels in its specifications, it is best to skip it.

## Material Safety and Non-Toxic Fabrics

When a sauna blanket heats up, any chemicals in the fabric can off-gas, meaning you could end up inhaling toxic fumes.

*   **Exterior and Interior Materials:** Look for blankets made with high-quality, waterproof polyurethane (PU) leather. It is more heat-resistant, durable, and softer than cheap PVC.
*   **Certifications:** Check if the materials are certified non-toxic. Look for designations like RoHS (Restriction of Hazardous Substances) or REACH certification, which ensure the materials are free from harmful heavy metals and flame retardants.
*   **Cleanability:** You will sweat heavily. The interior must be non-porous and waterproof so you can easily wipe it down with a damp cloth and mild soap or a vinegar-water solution after every session. Avoid fabrics that absorb moisture.

## Temperature Range and Zoning

Different blankets offer different levels of heat control.

*   **Temperature Range:** A good blanket should reach at least 140°F to 160°F (60°C to 71°C). Some go up to 176°F (80°C). If you are new to infrared therapy, you will want to start around 110°F (43°C) and gradually work your way up.
*   **Heating Zones:** Standard blankets have a single heating zone, meaning the entire blanket heats to the same temperature. Premium models feature dual-zone heating, allowing you to set a different temperature for your upper body and lower body. This is highly useful if your feet get uncomfortably hot but you still want deep heat on your back and shoulders.

## Closure Systems and Sizing

How the blanket opens and closes affects both heat retention and usability.

*   **Velcro vs. Zippers:** Industrial zippers are generally superior. They seal the heat in much more effectively than Velcro. Velcro can also catch on your clothing or towel and tends to collect hair and lint over time, making it harder to clean.
*   **Sizing:** Standard blankets are usually around 71 inches long and 32 inches wide (folded). If you are taller than 6 feet or have a broader build, look for "XL" models which offer extra width (usually around 36 inches) to prevent you from feeling too restricted. Remember, you need enough room to wrap yourself in a cotton towel inside the blanket to absorb sweat.

## Safety Features to Insist On

Never buy a sauna blanket that lacks basic electrical safety features.

*   **Auto-Shutoff Timer:** The controller should feature an automatic shutoff timer, typically adjustable between 30 and 60 minutes. This prevents the blanket from overheating if you accidentally fall asleep.
*   **Overheat Protection:** The control box should have an internal thermal sensor that automatically cuts power to the heating elements if the blanket exceeds its maximum safe operating temperature.
