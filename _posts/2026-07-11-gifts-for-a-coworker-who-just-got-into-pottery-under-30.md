If your coworker has recently taken up pottery, they are likely obsessed, constantly covered in a fine layer of gray dust, and spending all their free time at a local studio. Pottery is an expensive hobby to start, but you do not need to spend a fortune to get them a thoughtful gift. 

Most beginners start with a cheap, generic starter kit provided by their studio. Upgrading their everyday essentials makes a massive difference in their studio experience. Here are five practical, highly useful gifts for a beginner potter, all under $30.

## The Ultimate Studio Sponge

Most starter kits come with a cheap, yellow synthetic sponge that degrades quickly, shreds on rough clay, and holds too much water. [Mudtools](https://mudtools.com/) makes professional-grade ceramic tools, and their MudSponges are legendary among potters. 

Unlike household sponges, these are made of a durable, tight-celled polymer material that wears down slowly and finished clay surfaces beautifully. 
* **The Blue "Workhorse" Sponge (~$8):** Excellent for throwing on the wheel and absorbing water from the bottom of pots.
* **The Orange "Finishing" Sponge (~$8):** Smooths out finger ridges and refines rims.

Gifting a bundle of one blue and one orange sponge costs under $20, but it provides an immediate, noticeable upgrade to their daily throwing sessions.

## A Split-Leg Pottery Apron

Standard kitchen aprons do not work well at the pottery wheel. When a potter sits down and spreads their legs to frame the wheel head, a normal apron rides up, leaving their thighs and knees completely exposed to flying clay splatters and water. 

A split-leg apron (sometimes called a French leg apron) features separate overlapping fabric panels for each leg. When sitting, the panels drape over each thigh individually, keeping pants completely clean. You can find durable canvas split-leg aprons online for $20 to $28. Look for one with adjustable criss-cross back straps (which relieve neck strain during long sessions) and at least one chest pocket to hold a needle tool or pencil.

## Rite in the Rain Waterproof Notebook

Keeping track of details is crucial in ceramics. Potters need to log glaze combinations, clay types, kiln firing temperatures, and shrinkage rates—clay typically shrinks about 10% to 15% from its wet state to its final fired state. 

However, paper notebooks rarely survive wet, muddy hands. A [Rite in the Rain](https://www.riteintherain.com/) waterproof pocket notebook (around $10 to $15) solves this problem. Made with wood-based but chemically coated paper, these notebooks resist water, sweat, and mud. Your coworker can write down glaze recipes and dimensions with a standard pencil even if their hands are soaking wet or covered in clay slip. 

## Amaco Velvet Underglazes

Most community studios provide basic house glazes, but these are often limited to earth tones like brown, white, and gray. Underglazes work like paint, allowing potters to draw detailed designs, patterns, or illustrations directly onto raw clay. 

[Amaco](https://www.amaco.com/) Velvet Underglazes are the gold standard for beginners because they do not run or distort during the firing process. A couple of 2-ounce jars in vibrant colors—such as Flame Orange, Turquoise, or Bright Yellow—run about $8 to $12 each. Gifting two or three distinct colors gives your coworker the freedom to customize their pieces without relying on boring studio glazes.

## Heavy-Duty Hand Cream

Clay is naturally dehydrating. It acts as a desiccant, drawing moisture out of the skin. Working with wet clay, followed by washing hands repeatedly in cold studio buckets, strips the skin of its natural oils. Within a few weeks of starting pottery, most beginners complain of dry, chalky, and painfully cracked hands. 

A tub of O’Keeffe’s Working Hands cream or a heavy-duty lanolin-based hand salve ($8 to $12) is an incredibly thoughtful, practical gift. These creams create a protective barrier that locks in moisture and helps skin recover overnight. It is a small, caring gesture that they will use after every single studio session.
