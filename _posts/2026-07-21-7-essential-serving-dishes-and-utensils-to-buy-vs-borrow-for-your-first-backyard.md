Hosting your first backyard potluck is one of the easiest ways to entertain a crowd, but outfitting your patio table from scratch gets expensive fast. Between heavy glass platters that might shatter on concrete pavers and giant serving bowls that eat up cabinet space 364 days a year, buying every piece brand new is not practical.

To keep your budget intact and your kitchen clutter-free, here is a practical breakdown of seven essential serving items for an outdoor [potluck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potluck)—and whether you should invest in your own or ask guests and neighbors to bring them.

## 1. Buy: 12-inch and 16-inch Stainless Steel Tongs
If you only buy one type of utensil for outdoor entertaining, make it two pairs of heavy-duty locking tongs. Cheap plastic serving tongs twist under weight and drop heavy items like grilled chicken breasts or ears of corn. Investing $15 to $20 in commercial-grade stainless steel tongs from a restaurant supply seller like [WebstaurantStore](https://www.webstaurantstore.com) gives you complete control over hot food and heavy salads alike. Look for spring-loaded models with rubberized grips and ring-locking mechanisms for easy drawer storage.

## 2. Borrow: Chafing Dishes and Sterno Sets
Chafing dishes keep large batches of pulled pork or baked beans warm for hours, but metal frames and water pans are bulky, expensive, and notoriously difficult to store. Unless you host 30-person gatherings every month, ask a neighbor or family member to borrow theirs. If nobody has one, ask guests bringing hot sides to bring them in a slow cooker—you can plug two or three into a heavy-duty outdoor power strip near your patio outlet.

## 3. Buy: 2-Gallon Beverage Dispenser with a Metal Spigot
Open punch bowls attract bugs outdoors, while individual cans and bottles create endless recycling trash. A 2-gallon glass or Tritan plastic beverage dispenser lets guests self-serve ice water, lemonade, or batch cocktails cleanly. Choose a model featuring a stainless steel spigot rather than a painted plastic flick valve, which tends to leak after two or three uses. A solid dispenser costs around $25 to $35 and will become a staple for every future outdoor gathering.

## 4. Borrow: Tiered Serving Risers and Platter Stands
Tiered stands look great on a buffet table because they elevate dishes and save horizontal space, but they are single-purpose items that take up huge amounts of shelf space in your pantry. Large ceramic platters are equally tricky; if a guest drops porcelain on a brick patio, you end up cleaning up sharp shards mid-party. Borrow these decorative pieces or use sturdy wooden cutting boards you already own to create height on your table.

## 5. Buy: Shatterproof Acrylic Salad Bowls with Lids
Large glass mixing bowls work fine indoors, but patio stone is unforgiving. A high-capacity (6- to 8-quart) acrylic or polycarbonate salad bowl is lightweight, drop-safe, and ideal for potato salads, pasta salads, or leafy greens. Look for sets that include a fitted snap-on lid. The lid keeps bugs off the food before guests arrive and lets you snap the bowl straight into the refrigerator when the party wraps up.

## 6. Borrow: Extra Coolers and Galvanized Tubs
You will always need more cold storage than you expect. Between ice, beer, canned sodas, and perishable side dishes, a single household cooler rarely cuts it. Don't buy a second or third 60-quart rolling cooler just for one afternoon. Ask two attending guests to bring an extra cooler loaded with ice. Friends are almost always happy to contribute cooler space in lieu of cooking a complex dish.

## 7. Buy: Thermal Insulated Casserole Carrier
When hosting, you need to hold hot dishes at safe temperatures while waiting for guests to assemble. An insulated fabric casserole tote (sized for standard 9x13-inch baking dishes) keeps hot food above 140°F for up to two hours without using electricity. Brands like [Pyrex](https://www.pyrexhome.com) manufacture durable carriers lined with high-density thermal insulation that double as transport bags when you attend potlucks at someone else's home later in the season.
