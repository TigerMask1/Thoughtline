Camping in cold weather—typically defined as temperatures dropping below 40°F (4°C)—is incredibly peaceful, but it leaves very little margin for error. If you pack the wrong gear, you won't just be uncomfortable; you could face hypothermia. To stay warm and safe on a two-night weekend trip, you need to focus on insulation, caloric intake, and moisture management.

Here is the highly specific gear list you need to pack for a successful cold-weather weekend.

## The Sleep System: Your Defense Against the Ground
Many campers blame their sleeping bag when they get cold at night, but the ground is usually the real culprit. Cold earth siphons your body heat away rapidly through conduction.

* **An Insulated Sleeping Pad Setup:** You need a sleeping pad with an [R-value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R-value_(insulation)) of 4.0 or higher. For winter conditions, a great trick is stacking pads. Put a closed-cell foam pad, like a [Therm-a-Rest](https://www.thermarest.com/) Z Lite (R-value 2.0), directly on the tent floor, and place an insulated inflatable pad (R-value 3.5 to 5.0) on top of it.
* **The Right Sleeping Bag:** Look for a bag rated at least 10°F to 15°F colder than the lowest temperature you expect to encounter. If the forecast says 30°F, pack a 15°F or 20°F bag. Remember that "survival ratings" are not "comfort ratings." Down insulation offers the best warmth-to-weight ratio, but synthetic insulation is safer if you expect wet conditions, as it still insulates when damp.

## The Three-Layer Clothing Rule
The key to staying warm during the day is managing sweat. If you sweat while hiking or setting up camp, that moisture will freeze when you stop moving. Pack these exact layers:

1. **Base Layer (Moisture Management):** Bring two sets of mid-weight (200g/m²) merino wool or synthetic thermal tops and bottoms. Never wear cotton, which holds moisture and robs you of heat. Keep one set clean and dry specifically for sleeping.
2. **Mid Layer (Insulation):** This traps your body heat. A high-loft fleece jacket or a lightweight down "puffy" jacket works best.
3. **Outer Layer (Weather Protection):** A breathable, waterproof, and windproof shell jacket and pants (like Gore-Tex). This protects your insulation layers from wind, rain, and snow.
* **Footwear and Extremities:** Pack three pairs of heavyweight merino wool socks (one pair per day, plus one dedicated dry sleeping pair). Bring a wind-resistant beanie that covers your ears, and two pairs of gloves: a thin liner pair for dexterity and a heavy, waterproof outer pair.

## Cooking and Hydration in the Cold
Cold air is dry, and your body works harder in winter, making dehydration a real risk. However, cold weather changes how your kitchen gear functions.

* **The Right Stove:** Standard canister stoves that use a butane/propane mix lose pressure and sputter when temperatures drop near freezing. If you expect sub-freezing temps, use a liquid-fuel stove (like white gas) or a canister stove with a pressure regulator.
* **Water Bottle Management:** Keep your water from freezing overnight by storing your bottles inside your tent, ideally wrapped in extra clothing or inside an insulated sleeve. Use wide-mouth bottles (like a 1-liter [Nalgene](https://www.nalgene.com/) bottle), as narrow spouts freeze shut easily. Store them upside down; water freezes from the top down, so this keeps the threads ice-free.

## Crucial Cold-Weather Extras

* **The Hot Water Bottle Trick:** Before going to bed, boil water and pour it into a hard-sided Nalgene bottle. Ensure the lid is screwed on tightly, wrap it in a spare sock, and place it at the foot of your sleeping bag. It will radiate heat for up to eight hours.
* **Keep Electronics Warm:** Cold temperatures drain batteries rapidly. Keep your phone, headlamp, and portable power banks inside your inner jacket pockets during the day, and sleep with them inside your sleeping bag at night.
* **High-Fat, High-Calorie Foods:** Your body burns significantly more calories in the cold just to maintain its core temperature. Pack dense, easy-to-eat foods like nuts, cheese, peanut butter, and chocolate. Eat a high-fat snack right before bed to keep your internal furnace burning through the night.
