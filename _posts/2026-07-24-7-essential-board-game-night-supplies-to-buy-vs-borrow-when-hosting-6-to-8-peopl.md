Hosting a game night for six to eight people is a sweet spot for tabletop gaming, but it puts unique demands on your home. Most standard dining tables comfortably seat four to six, and typical strategy games rarely accommodate eight without long, boring wait times between turns. To avoid overspending on single-use gear or overcrowding your room, you need to know which supplies are worth buying permanently and which you should borrow from your guests.

## 1. Extra Folding Chairs: BORROW

A group of eight requires more seating than most apartments or small dining rooms have standard. Do not spend $80 to $100 buying a set of metal folding chairs that will clutter your closet eleven months out of the year. Instead, ask two of your attending friends to bring canvas camp chairs or padded folding chairs. Camping chairs are often more comfortable for a three-hour gaming session than cheap plastic utility chairs, and they fold up instantly when the night ends.

## 2. A Neoprene Table Mat: BUY

If you plan to host tabletop games more than twice a year, buy a 3x5-foot or 4x6-foot rollable [neoprene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoprene) play mat. Bare wood and glass tables make picking up thin playing cards frustrating, and hard surfaces cause dice to bounce onto the floor. A basic desk mat or rubber-backed felt cover provides traction, dampens noisy dice rolls, and protects your table surface from accidental liquid rings. Unbranded rubber play mats cost around $25 to $35 and can easily be trimmed to fit your table size.

## 3. High-Capacity Games for 6 to 8 Players: BUY

Most popular strategy games max out at four players. Forcing an eight-player session using base-game expansions often results in 20-minute gaps between turns. Own two or three dedicated high-capacity games that keep eight people active simultaneously. Games like *Codenames* from [Czech Games Edition](https://czechgames.com/en/codenames/), *7 Wonders* (which plays up to seven with zero extra downtime), or social deduction games like *Secret Hitler* keep the energy high across a larger group without stalling out.

## 4. Dice Rolling Trays: BUY

With eight people gathered around one table, wild dice rolls will inevitably knock over player pieces, flip over cards, or end up in someone's drink. A two-pack of snap-together faux leather or felt dice trays costs under $15. Placing one tray at each end of the table keeps dice contained and saves players from having to lean awkwardly across three people just to make a roll.

## 5. Secondary Table Surface: BORROW

An eight-player setup requires roughly 15 to 20 square feet of usable surface once you account for individual player boards, the central board, drinks, and scorecards. If your dining table measures under six feet long, ask a guest to bring a six-foot plastic folding tailgate table or a 3x3-foot card table. Butt the secondary table against your main table or use it as a dedicated side station for rulebooks, overflow components, and drinks.

## 6. Silicone Component Dishes: BUY

Games designed for larger groups feature hundreds of resource tokens, coins, and wooden markers. Dumping these into messy piles on the table slows down play and leads to dropped pieces. Buy a set of 8 to 12 flexible silicone muffin cups or collapsible felt bit bowls for around $10. They hold components neatly, can be passed down the table easily, and squeeze flat into game boxes when you are done.

## 7. Serving Bowls and Snack Trays: BORROW

Feeding eight people takes up valuable table space and requires multiple food stations so players do not have to stretch across the board. Rather than buying extra platter sets, ask guests to bring their snacks already placed in their own serving bowls or containers. This saves your kitchen prep space, spreads food out evenly across both ends of the table, and eliminates extra dishwashing at midnight.
