Use these simple templates and tips to write a sincere, respectful sympathy note to a coworker, neighbor, or friend.", "image_query": "writing condolence card pen", "article_markdown": "Writing a sympathy card is already tough, but it feels especially tricky when you didn't know the deceased person. Maybe you are writing to a coworker who lost a parent, a neighbor whose spouse passed away, or a close friend whose sibling you never met. You want to show support without sounding fake or pretending you had a bond that didn't exist.\n\nThe secret to writing these notes is shifting your focus. You aren't writing a eulogy for the person who died; you are writing a message of comfort to the person who is grieving. Here is how to strike the right tone.\n\n## The Three-Step Formula for Brief Sympathy Notes\n\nWhen you don't have personal anecdotes to share, stick to a structured three-step formula. This keeps your note sincere, concise, and easy to write.\n\n1. **Acknowledge the loss directly.** State clearly that you are sorry to hear about their loss. Avoid euphemisms like \"passed on\" or \"went to a better place,\" which can sometimes ring hollow.\n2. **Validate their relationship.** Since you didn't know the deceased, focus on the person you do know. Mention how much they mean to you, or acknowledge the deep bond they shared with their loved one.\n3. **Offer a gentle closing.** End with a warm, low-pressure sign-off.\n\nAccording to traditional [etiquette guidelines from the Emily Post Institute](https://emilypost.com), a handwritten note is always preferred over an email or text message, even if it is only two or three sentences long.\n\n## Specific Wording Examples for Different Situations\n\nHere are concrete templates you can adapt based on your relationship with the survivor.\n\n### For a Coworker or Professional Contact\n\nIf a colleague is grieving, you want to offer comfort while maintaining professional boundaries.\n\n* **Option 1:** \"Dear [Name], I was so sorry to hear about the passing of your [parent]. While I never had the chance to meet them, I know how much they meant to you. Please know that your team is thinking of you, and we are here to support you when you return.\"\n* **Option 2:** \"Dear [Name], Please accept my deepest condolences on the loss of your [spouse]. I am keeping you and your family in my thoughts during this difficult time. Don't worry about anything at the office; we have your projects covered.\"\n\n### For a Neighbor or Casual Acquaintance\n\nKeep these notes warm, neighborly, and offer practical, local support if appropriate.\n\n* **Option 1:** \"Dear [Name], I was deeply saddened to hear about the passing of [Deceased's Name]. Although we only crossed paths occasionally, I always appreciated their warm smile in the neighborhood. Please let me know if I can help rake leaves, take out the trash, or run any errands for you this month.\"\n* **Option 2:** \"Dear [Name], Sending you my warmest thoughts during this sad time. I am so sorry for your loss. I am just a few doors down if you need anything at all.\"\n\n### For a Close Friend's Relative You Never Met\n\nWhen your friend loses a loved one you didn't know, your main goal is to support your friend's grief.\n\n* **Option 1:** \"Dear [Name], I am so incredibly sorry for the loss of your sister. I know how close you two were and how much you loved her. I am here for you to talk, cry, or just sit in silence whenever you are ready.\"\n* **Option 2:** \"Dear [Name], Holding you close in my heart as you navigate the loss of your grandfather. Even though I never met him, I know how much he shaped the person you are today. I'm bringing dinner over on Thursday—no need to host, I'll just drop it on the porch.\"\n\n## What to Avoid Writing\n\nWhen writing to someone you don't know well, it is easy to accidentally say the wrong thing while trying to be helpful. Avoid these common pitfalls:\n\n* **Don't say \"I know how you feel.\"** Even if you have experienced a similar loss, everyone's grief journey is unique.\n* **Don't ask how they died.** Curiosity can feel invasive during a highly sensitive time.\n* **Don't offer empty platitudes.** Phrases like \"Everything happens for a reason\" can minimize the survivor's very real pain. If you are unsure what to write, sticking to a simple \"I am so sorry for your loss\" is always safe. You can read more about the history and social customs of expressing grief on [Wikipedia's condolences page](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condolences)."​}
